Design Thinking toolkit

Summary

As part of my traineeship, I worked on improving the adoption of the Design Thinking Toolkit within the organization. User testing revealed that while people were interested in using the toolkit, they struggled to understand when and how to apply it. To address this, I developed a new support tool—the Eilandbord (Island Board)—to help employees see Design Thinking as a structured, holistic process rather than a loose collection of activities.

Context

The Design Thinking Toolkit was created to spread Design Thinking practices across the organization. Design Thinking offers a simple, human-centered approach to problem-solving, focusing on user needs and encouraging employees to take ownership of change and innovation, they wouldn’t need the Innovation Lab help anymore.
However, early user testing showed gaps in adoption: people were unsure about use cases, suitable challenges, and how to select methods or steps. For this project, the scope was narrowed to early adopters, with the goal of equipping them to inspire others later.

Process

Empathize

The differences between employees who embraced experimentation and those who didn’t feel empowered to try were identified.
Learned that many saw Design Thinking as just a set of exercises, not a full process.

Define

Focused on two central questions:
How do we make colleagues aware that Design Thinking can be part of their current work?
How do we communicate Design Thinking as an integrated, end-to-end process?

Ideate

Facilitated group brainstorms that generated effective ideas for both the toolkit and future organizational adoption.

Prototype

Different version of a paper prototype that were tested and changed again to a final prototype. Along with a digital version that was made

Test

Different user test done at different stages with different prototypes

Advice

The main advice that was given was to use the map in conversation as a visual aid. As well as continue to make others enthusiatic about Design Thinking in general

Final design

The Eilandbord helps project teams and their clients plan and visualize Design Thinking phases. By laying out steps as a journey across the island, it makes the process feel cohesive and concrete. The tool is supported by a facilitator’s guide and can be used independently or during workshops.

Reflection

This project showed me how important it is to balance tools with mindset. A toolkit alone doesn’t drive change—people need clarity, confidence, and space to experiment. By translating abstract methodology into tangible, visual tools, I learned how to make Design Thinking more approachable and actionable for a wide audience.
